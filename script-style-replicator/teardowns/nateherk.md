## THE REPEATABLE VIRAL FORMULA (identical across every hit below)

### The copyable BEAT SKELETON
Every hit runs the same 7 beats. It is a **screen-recording walk-through wrapped in a result → CTA sandwich**:

1. **RESULT HOOK** — the finished outcome stated as accomplished fact, value-noun by ~word 7, a number/$/"free"/"instantly" baked in.
2. **THE REVEAL / "not what you assume"** — the twist that opens the curiosity gap ("this website is not Apple").
3. **ENABLER NAME-DROP** — the one skill/file/tool that makes it possible. This name *is* the future keyword.
4. **NARRATED WALK-THROUGH** — present-tense screen narration, partial not complete (gate the how).
5. **REACTION PAYOFF** — a live "it worked" beat ("boom," "Wow, I mean…").
6. **ZOOM-OUT / RECEIPT** — one line on why it matters or a third-person proof number.
7. **CTA** — gate the depth to the full breakdown / community / keyword.

### The EXACT transitional phrases reused VERBATIM across videos
These are the load-bearing connective tissue. Counts are across the four teardown videos plus the wider 11-transcript sample.

- **"So,"** as a sentence-opener — his single most frequent word after articles. "So, here you can see…", "So, it can search…", "So, let me show you guys…", "So, real quick…", "So, in just a few minutes…". Appears **50+ times** across the four scripts. This is the nateherk metronome.
- **"All right, so…"** — hard scene-change / new-step marker. "All right, so I'm going to walk you guys through this process step-by-step…" ($10k sites); "All right, so before we get into the tutorials…" (99% cheaper); "All right, so that has finished up." (5 hacks).
- **"And now what it's doing is it's [verb]-ing…"** — the present-tense live-demo frame. "And now what it's doing is it's creating the Google Doc." (Google GWS); "And now what it's going to do is it should hopefully invoke the front-end design skill…" (5 hacks).
- **"And we can see boom."** / **"you can see"** — the reaction-payoff trigger. "And we can see boom. We have an actual resource guide." (Google GWS). "you can see" alone fires **20+ times** as the ever-present ostension verb ("you can see right now that this video…", "you can see it's a little bit longer now").
- **"Okay, so real quick, how did [X] actually [happen]?"** — the retention pivot into mechanism. "Okay, so real quick, how did this video actually get made?" (Fable 5); "so, real quick, in case you're not super deep into the AI space, let me give you guys some context…" (Millionaires).
- **"But anyways, that's going to do it for this one."** — the outro seal, near-verbatim in every long-form.
- **"If you want to watch the full breakdown, then click on that play button right here, and I'll see you guys over there."** — the shorts CTA, **verbatim in 6 of 7 shorts** (a fixed template, not improvised).
- **"So, if you guys enjoyed or you learned something new, please give it a like. It definitely helps me out a ton. And as always, I appreciate you guys making it to the end of the video. I'll see you on the next one. Thanks, guys."** — the long-form outro, so fixed that Nate points out his own AI avatar reproduced it word-for-word (Fable 5).

### The DICTION FINGERPRINT (the words he leans on EVERY time)
- **Second person, constant:** "you guys" is the default address (**40+ instances** across the four). "you can see," "you'll notice," "some of you guys have realized," "you guys can all do that." He never lectures a faceless audience; he narrates *to* one person he calls "you guys."
- **Present progressive for the live demo:** "it's creating," "it's asking us," "it's downloading," "the blender starts to appear," "the fruit is starting to appear." The tense makes a pre-recorded screen feel like it's happening now.
- **Filler-as-authenticity:** "literally," "obviously," "honestly," "basically," "kind of," "I mean," "you know." "literally" appears **12+ times** ("It literally just looks like raw markdown," "I literally just opened up Claude Fable," "literally driving a browser," "literally just files"). These are deliberate, not noise: they read as an unscripted real person.
- **Intensifier stacking by repetition:** "super, super easy," "so, so important," "really, really smart," "very, very high," "months and months." Doubling the adverb is his emphasis engine (no em-dash needed).
- **Contractions everywhere:** "here's," "it's," "you're," "I'm," "don't," "that's," "we've got." Near-zero uncontracted forms. Speed and casualness.
- **Sensory / physical verbs for software:** "drop that into Claude Code," "drag it over here," "chuck a doc in there," "shoot this off," "brain dump," "word vomit," "destroyed that / I ruined the skill," "supercharged," "sharpen the axe," "chop down a tree." Abstract software actions get concrete, tactile, sometimes violent verbs.
- **Numbers as texture, not decoration:** a figure lands every few sentences. "$10,000," "$65 billion," "$965 billion," "$47 billion," "47 times," "20 bucks," "$200 a month," "$10 per million / 50 on the output," "400,000 tokens," "40%," "in 30 minutes," "in 5 minutes," "95% of people," "60 or 70% right." Rule of thumb he obeys: **a number in the hook AND a number in the payoff.**
- **"boom" / "Wow" / "Nice." / "Ooh" reaction fragments** — the dopamine punctuation. Hard fragments against long run-ons.
- **Deliberately LEFT OUT:** the full prompt, the exact config, the file contents. He voices the WHAT ("it's making a Google Doc via a bash command") and withholds the HOW (the actual command / skill file), which is what the CTA sells.

### The VIRALITY LEVERS he pulls every single time (ranked by recurrence)
1. **Result-first open loop.** The finished thing exists in sentence one, so the viewer's only remaining question is "how," and that unanswered question is the retention engine.
2. **Specificity as credibility.** Named tools, named companies, named dollar figures, named people. Never "a lot" — always "1100 nodes," "99% property accuracy," "over half the batch."
3. **Novelty peg.** "new tool," "just dropped," "finally here," "new skill," "new generation." Every hook claims *this is new*, which manufactures urgency and share-worthiness.
4. **Show-don't-claim receipt.** The artifact is on screen; the VO points at it ("boom, we have an actual resource guide"). Proof is visual, so claims feel verified.
5. **Gated depth = engineered curiosity.** Shows the result + partial method, withholds the full recipe → the CTA is the natural resolution of the gap he built.
6. **Contrarian / undercut reframe.** "finally here. Or is it really?", "this website is not Apple," "Not via API call. Not via MCP. But via a bash command." He sets an expectation and flips it to re-hook.
