# gregisenberg — Script DNA
> Source stamp: 8 transcripts, 82,000–734,000 views (top outliers), fetched 2026-07-12. No prior hand-written ref — this is the first profile, grounded 100% in the transcripts.

## 0. Voiceprint
A calm builder-explainer who reframes a tool with one provocation, then walks you down a numbered "Start by… Next… Finally…" staircase where every step is an imperative that names a concrete artifact and its micro-payoff, and closes on a single transformation line ("turn a 4-hour task into a single instruction").

---

## 1. Opener / Hook Formula

### Primary template (used, in spirit, by 8 of 8)
> **[Provocation or number-promise that reframes a familiar tool] → "Here's [the setup / what you need to do]."**

The opener does two jobs in ~2 sentences: (1) destabilize the viewer's current assumption about a tool, and (2) promise a concrete setup. The explicit pivot phrase **"Here's how / Here's the setup / Here is how / Here's what you need to do"** appears verbatim in **3 of 8** (V1, V3, V8) and is implied by the immediate step-list in the other 5.

### The 4 opener sub-types (each maps to the same template)
1. **Rhetorical provocation** — "Why would you subscribe to Claude only to use 10% of its brain? Here's a setup that makes it worth the buy." (*4 .md files, 734K*)
2. **Conditional reframe** — "If you take notes, Claude just became your best thinking partner." (*Obsidian, 597K*)
3. **"Everyone's doing it wrong"** — "Everyone's using Claude code, like it's a magic wand… and wonder why the output still feels like slop. Here's what you need to do instead." (*Claude Code Explained, 419K*)
4. **Number/outcome promise** — "You can use [Claude] Code to launch 100 Facebook ads in under 30 minutes." (*100 FB ads, 90K*) · "Okay, so now you can also make money using OpenClaw. Here is how." (*OpenClaw money, 82K*)

### Variant openers
- **Analogy cold-open** — "Imagine a third date where they ask you your name again. That's how little Open Claw knows you, but Hermes is different." (*Hermes, 86K*)
- **"X recently dropped Y which is basically [analogy]"** — "Anthropic recently dropped Claude co-work which is basically Claude code but for non-coders." (*Cowork, 102K*)
- **Contrarian thesis** (podcast-clip, first-person) — "the future of building startups is not by building a startup, it's by building a micro startup." (*Micro-startups, 300K*)

### The 4 rules that make the hook fire
1. **Name the tool inside the first sentence** (Claude / Claude Code / Obsidian / OpenClaw / Hermes appears by word ~8 in 8/8).
2. **Attack the viewer's status quo** — you're using 10% of its brain / it feels like slop / it forgets your name. The hook implies you're currently doing it wrong or leaving value on the table.
3. **Put a concrete number or timeframe in the promise when possible** — "100 ads in under 30 minutes", "10% of its brain", "under 1 minute".
4. **Immediately pivot to the setup** — never linger; the second sentence is the "Here's how" doorway into the steps.

### 5 verbatim opener lines
1. "Why would you subscribe to Claude only to use 10% of its brain? Here's a setup that makes it worth the buy." (*4 .md files, 734K*)
2. "If you take notes, Claude just became your best thinking partner." (*Obsidian, 597K*)
3. "Everyone's using Claude code, like it's a magic wand. They say, 'Make me a meditation app,' and wonder why the output still feels like slop. Here's what you need to do instead." (*Claude Code Explained, 419K*)
4. "You can use [Claude] Code to launch 100 Facebook ads in under 30 minutes." (*100 FB ads, 90K*)
5. "Imagine a third date where they ask you your name again. That's how little Open Claw knows you, but Hermes is different." (*Hermes, 86K*)

## 2. Structural Skeleton (the beat map)

Greg's shorts are a **numbered how-to staircase** bookended by a reframe hook and a transformation line.

### Section-label grammar
He does **not** use hard section titles (unlike raycfu's "The Scanner."). His labels are **ordinal connectors that act as invisible chapter markers**: `Start by… / First… / Next… / Then… / After that… / But… / Finally… / Lastly… / In the end…`. Every video runs 4–6 of these in strict sequence.

### Ordered beats
| # | Beat | Function | Verbatim example |
|---|------|----------|------------------|
| 1 | **Reframe hook** | Destabilize + name tool | "Why would you subscribe to Claude only to use 10% of its brain?" |
| 2 | **Setup pivot** | Promise the how | "Here's a setup that makes it worth the buy." / "Here's what you need to do instead." |
| 3 | **Step 1 (foundation)** | First artifact + what it is | "Start by creating the agents.md file. It's what you would first give an employee while onboarding them." |
| 4 | **Steps 2–4 (build-up)** | Each: imperative → artifact → micro-benefit | "Next is the context folder. This contains heavier and more nuanced information." |
| 5 | **The "But" escalation** | Re-hook: name the real magic / objection | "But feeding context alone isn't enough. It also needs to remember your preferences." |
| 6 | **Final step** | Last capability, often the biggest | "And lastly, create the skills folder where you literally walk through a process once…" |
| 7 | **Transformation line** | Payoff / vision close | "That's how you can turn a 4-hour task into a single instruction." |

### Target length / word budget
138–181 words (avg ~155), delivered as a "short." All 8 land in this band. For Alex at 35–45s that maps to **~150–190 words** — a native fit, no compression needed.

## 4. Sentence Rhythm & Mechanics

- **Avg sentence length:** ~10–11 words. (V1 = 144 words / 14 sentences = 10.3 w/s.) Steps are short (5–9 words: "Next is the context folder.") with an occasional long compound step (16–23 words) right before the payoff.
- **Opener length:** ~11 words avg (V1=12, V3 first clause=8, V5=14, V6=13, V7=11, V8=10).
- **Imperative vs declarative mix:** Hook + payoff are declarative/interrogative; the entire middle is **imperative-dominant**. Step verbs across the set: *Start by, create, use, break it, watch, download, connect, ask, build, generate, feed, push, deploy, map out, set, hook, stack, run, install, grant.* Roughly one imperative per step, 4–6 per video.
- **Number/$ density — HIGH:** "10%", "4-hour", "single instruction", "100 Facebook ads / under 30 minutes", "200,000 tokens", "40 to 50%", "$100 token burn into $10", "5 to 10 micro startups", "under 1 minute". At least one hard number in 8/8, usually 2–3.
- **Causal connectors:** **"But" is the signature pivot** (used 7+ times as a re-hook: "But first," "But feeding context alone isn't enough," "But you can even take it a notch higher," "But what about the elephant in the room?", "But that's not all," "But, what's even cooler," "But, a better way"). "So" gates conclusions ("So, whenever you hit the 40 to 50% mark…"). "Then" / "Next" carry sequence.
- **Signature punctuation/cadence:** Short interrogative or fragment as a spotlight ("But what about the elephant in the room? Safety." / "The best part?"). Comma-stacked triads to concretize ("your business, voice, and how you like to work" · "the scraping, the formatting, and the uploading"). **No em-dashes present in any transcript** — he uses commas and periods, which aligns natively with Alex's rule 1.

## 6. Retention / Escalation Devices

1. **The "But" re-hook** — the single most-used device. Right when a step feels complete, "But [X] alone isn't enough" or "But what's even cooler" resets curiosity and promises a deeper layer (7+ occurrences).
2. **Fragment spotlights** — one-word or two-word beats that jolt pacing: "Safety." · "The best part?"
3. **Number-drops mid-video** — a concrete stat inside a step re-anchors credibility ("AI starts forgetting your instructions at around 50%," "turning a $100 token burn into $10").
4. **Escalating stakes across steps** — steps are ordered small → big; the last step is the most powerful ("And lastly, create the skills folder… turn a 4-hour task into a single instruction").
5. **The final transformation line** — every video pays off the hook's promise with a clean before→after, giving a reason to have watched to the end.
6. **"You can even take it a notch higher"** — an explicit bonus-beat that signals extra value beyond the baseline.

## 7. Proof & Credibility Style

- **Concrete artifacts, named** — agents.md, context folder, memory.md, skills folder, Obsidian CLI, `today`/`emerge` commands, Perplexity API, Facebook Ads API, Railway, Open Router, Qwen, cron jobs, virtual machines, value-effort matrix, AutoCAD. Specificity is the proof.
- **Hard numbers as receipts** — "100 ads / 30 minutes", "$100 → $10", "50% context", "4-hour → single instruction".
- **Analogy-as-proof** — makes the abstract legible: onboarding an employee, a magic wand, a third date, a personal command center, "notes become its brain."
- **Light third-person / team credibility** — "We also tested how Claude Co-work would work with professional softwares like AutoCAD." He rarely tells a personal war story; proof is the demoed capability, not an anecdote (the one exception is the first-person podcast clip, *Micro-startups*).
- **Name-drops** are tool/vendor names (Anthropic, Obsidian, Perplexity, Railway), never influencer flexes.

## 8. CTA Formula

**Important — Greg's shorts have NO comment-keyword CTA.** They end cold on the **transformation line** ("That's how you can turn a 4-hour task into a single instruction." / "And that's how Claude connects the dots for you."). The payoff sentence IS the close; there is no "comment X" and no follow/like ask in any of the 8.

- Native Greg close template → **"That's how you can [BEFORE → AFTER]."** or **"And that's how [TOOL] [does the dream] for you."**
- For Alex this is the biggest graft point (see §10): keep Greg's transformation-line rhythm but **replace the vision tail with a gated keyword CTA and cut hard on the keyword.**

## 9. Why It Works (transferable mechanism)

- **Reframe-then-relieve:** the hook makes you feel behind ("using 10% of its brain"), the staircase relieves it. Tension → resolution in 30s.
- **The numbered staircase is frictionless** — "Start by… Next… Finally…" gives the brain a progress bar; you stay for the last step because it's flagged as the biggest.
- **Every step is imperative + artifact + micro-payoff** — it feels immediately doable, never abstract, and never jargon-locked (each artifact gets a plain-English or analogy gloss).
- **The "But" pivots manufacture second and third curiosity gaps** so a 155-word script has 3 mini-hooks, not one.
- **The payoff line rewards completion** with a crisp before→after, which is the shareable/screenshot moment.

## 10. Adapting to Alex (the fusion layer)

### What transfers cleanly
- **The reframe hook** (rhetorical provocation / number-promise) — perfect for a faceless mascot VO; put the value noun by word ~12.
- **The "Start by… Next… Finally…" staircase** — ideal for burn-in captions synced to screen-recordings; each step = one caption card.
- **The "But [real magic]" escalation** and **fragment spotlights** — natural VO retention beats.
- **No em-dashes** — Greg already writes this way; zero friction with Alex rule 1.
- **Number/outcome/timeframe density** — matches Alex's outcome-framing (money/hours/customers).
- **Analogy glosses** — keep the 12-year-old parse test satisfied.

### Conflicts with Alex's HARD RULES → exact resolution
1. **Greg hands over the FULL replicable method (rule 3, gate-the-how).** Greg literally spells out every file and step. **Resolution:** keep the step cadence but let each step **name the artifact and sell the result** without giving the complete prompt/config. The full prompts/system get gated behind the comment keyword. (e.g. "Start with a Skill that learns your refund rules and tone" — named + sold, not fully specified.)
2. **Greg ends on a vision line, not a keyword CTA (rule 4, cut-on-keyword).** **Resolution:** compress Greg's transformation line into a third-person receipt, then append a one-line gated CTA and **end hard on the keyword** ("…Comment SUPPORT."). No "and I'll send it over" tail.
3. **The *Micro-startups* clip is first-person ("I actually think…") (rule 2, no first-person anecdote).** **Resolution:** drop first-person entirely; convert any "I did X" into "you"-framed instruction or a **third-person receipt** ("one founder turned a 4-hour inbox into a 10-minute review").
4. **Breadth litmus (rule 7).** Some Greg topics skew technical (Hermes on a terminal, Qwen routing). **Resolution:** pick artifacts a designer, Shopify owner, coach, AND founder can all use (support agent, content Skill, inbox loop), and keep every step's gloss jargon-free.

### Gate-the-how mapping for this format
- **Shown in VO:** the reframe, the *names* of the artifacts (Skill, memory file, context folder, overnight loop), the outcome/number, one analogy gloss per artifact, the before→after receipt.
- **Gated behind keyword:** the actual prompts, the exact file contents, the loop config, the connection steps. The VO says "the exact Skill and memory file" and points to the comment keyword.
