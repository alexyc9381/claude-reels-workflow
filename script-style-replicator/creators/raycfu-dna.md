## 0. Voiceprint
A calm, confident insider leaning across the table to hand you one specific secret: "Most people don't realize [surprising number]… here's how you build it," then walks you through 3–5 crisply named steps, each with a why and a dollar figure, and gates the actual prompts behind a one-word comment.

## 1. Opener / Hook Formula

**Primary template (5 of 8, and the 417K top outlier uses it):**
> "Most people don't realize that [SURPRISING CAPABILITY + NUMBER/OUTCOME]."

- V1 (417K): "Most people don't realize that they can set up 4[] agents to work together as the dev team to ship out features while you are asleep."
- V3 (54.5K): "Most people don't realize the reason that the resume gets rejected is because it gets filtered out by a robot called the applicant tracking system before a human ever sees it."
- V4 (50K): "Most people don't realize that there's over 60,000 cloud skills. I tested over 100 of them and here's the only ones that matter."
- V6 (36.4K): "Most people don't realize that quant traders never trust a strategy the first time that it works."
- V8 (29K): "Most people don't realize that all you need is Claude's skills and detail prompts in order to create an entire product to sell online."

**Variant A — "Nobody is talking about this, but…" (2 of 8):** same secret-knowledge frame, sharper contrarian bite.
- V2 (61K): "Nobody is talking about this, but the traditional agency model of 10 employees charging $50,000 per project is completely obsolete."
- V7 (30K): "Nobody talks about this, but there's a team running 20 AI agents inside Cloud Code to generate their clients 10 million dollars in revenue."

**Variant B — "You need to pay attention because…" (1 of 8):** urgency + authority-drop.
- V5 (40K): "You need to pay attention because cloud is giving away a free AI certification that Deloitte is paying thousands of employees just to get."

So **8 of 8 openers are a secret-knowledge / insider-gap frame** (curiosity gap = "here's what most people miss"). The exact three-word lead is interchangeable; the mechanism is identical.

**The rules that make the hook fire:**
1. **One sentence, ~21 words, one surprising claim.** No throat-clearing, no "hey guys." The first word is the hook word.
2. **A number or dollar figure lives inside the hook** (60,000 skills, $50,000/project, 20 agents → $10M, 4 agents). 7 of 8 hooks carry a number.
3. **The value noun lands by ~word 9–12** ("dev team," "resume gets rejected," "60,000 cloud skills," "customer inbox").
4. **It flatters the viewer as about-to-be-an-insider** — the enemy is "most people," and you are being let past them.
5. **The payoff is concrete and outcome-framed** (money, hours, a job, features shipped while you sleep), never abstract capability.

**5 verbatim opener lines:**
1. "Most people don't realize that they can set up 4 agents to work together as the dev team to ship out features while you are asleep." (V1, 417K)
2. "Nobody is talking about this, but the traditional agency model of 10 employees charging $50,000 per project is completely obsolete." (V2, 61K)
3. "Most people don't realize the reason that the resume gets rejected is because it gets filtered out by a robot… before a human ever sees it." (V3, 54.5K)
4. "You need to pay attention because cloud is giving away a free AI certification that Deloitte is paying thousands of employees just to get." (V5, 40K)
5. "Nobody talks about this, but there's a team running 20 AI agents inside Cloud Code to generate their clients 10 million dollars in revenue." (V7, 30K)

## 2. Structural Skeleton (the beat map)

**The section-label grammar — raycfu's signature "The [Role]." headers.** He chunks the body into short **noun-phrase labels** spoken as their own sentence, which act as chapter titles and micro curiosity-gaps:
- V3: "The scanner." … "The Surgeon." … "The Stress Test." … "The sparring partner."
- V2: "The niche." … "The tech stack." … "The client acquisition."
- V8: "Prompt 1." … "Prompt 2." … (numbered variant)
- V4: "For marketing," … "Design." … "For developers," … "For everyone," (category variant)
- V7: "in three steps" then an implicit sequence.

**Ordered beats:**

| # | Beat | Function | Verbatim example |
|---|------|----------|------------------|
| 1 | **Hook** | Secret-gap + number, value noun early | "Most people don't realize that they can set up 4 agents… while you are asleep." (V1) |
| 2 | **Bridge / promise** | Restate payoff, count the steps | "This is how you set it up." (V1) · "This is how the system works in three steps." (V7) · "Here's how to build this out." (V6) |
| 3 | **The wrong way (optional)** | Myth-bust what most people do, to create contrast | "Most people come up with an idea, back test it once… two weeks later they are bleeding money." (V6) |
| 4 | **Named steps ×3–5** | Each: LABEL → what it does → **why it matters** → concrete example/number | "The Surgeon. …rewrite your experience using the Google xyz formula… Instead of 'manage the team' it becomes 'reduced customer churn by 18% over six months…'" (V3) |
| 5 | **The differentiator** | Spotlight the deepest step as the thing that separates this from everything else | "This is the most important part." (V1) · "This final part is the one that separates this content system from everything else." (V7) |
| 6 | **CTA / gated asset** | Offer the guide, gate behind a one-word comment | "I created a detailed guide with the exact prompts… comment higher and I'll send it to you." (V3) |
| 7 | **Sign-off (raycfu only)** | Follow-bait tail | "Follow for more advice on making money and running your business with tech and AI." → **Alex CUTS this (see §10).** |

**Target length / word budget:** 216–442 words in his outliers; the 417K top hit is a tight **295 words**. YouTube shorts run leaner (216–307), TikTok listicles longer (402–442). For Alex's 35–45s target, aim **140–175 words**.

## 4. Sentence Rhythm & Mechanics

- **Avg sentence length ≈ 15–20 words.** Explanatory, not staccato. He teaches, he doesn't bark. Sentences are compound with a because/so clause riding along.
- **Opener length ≈ 21 words** (range 10–31); the single longest sentence in the whole script is usually the hook or the differentiator explanation.
- **Imperative vs declarative:** body is **declarative-explanatory** framing steps as facts ("The coder agent reads the spec and builds exactly what it says"), with **imperatives clustered inside the how-to** ("Create 4 specialized agents." "Use Opus 4 as the driver." "Upload your resume." "Set up an agent that monitors job listings."). Roughly one imperative per named step. He tells you what it does more than he orders you around.
- **Number / $ density: very high — a number roughly every 2–3 sentences.** $80k/month, $50,000, 90%, $5–7K, 300 sub-agents, 80% cheaper, $3M, $10M, 20 agents, $500/day, 60,000 skills, 277,000 installs, 96,000 stars, 18%, 3,000 people, 200 resumes, $99, 5,000 people, 60 questions. Numbers are load-bearing, not decoration.
- **Causal connectors are the backbone: because / so / so that / instead / but.** Every step earns a "why." "Use Opus… because the quality of a plan sets the ceiling for everything else." "…writes to a shared folder… so that every agent has context." "Instead of 'manage the team' it becomes…" Kill the causal clause and the voice dies.
- **Cadence / punctuation:** period-driven, short-to-medium. The section labels are **sentence fragments used as headers** ("The Scanner.") — his single most identifiable cadence tic. Present tense throughout. No em-dashes in his ASR anyway (keeps Alex safe).

## 6. Retention / Escalation Devices

1. **Named-step labels as mini curiosity-gaps.** "The Surgeon." lands before he explains it, so you stay to find out what the Surgeon does. Each label re-hooks.
2. **The "most important part / where the magic happens" spotlight** re-centers attention right before the best beat (V1, V6, V7 all use it near the end).
3. **The differentiator beat** ("the one that separates this from everything else") is deliberately saved for last-content-beat position — the payoff you waited for.
4. **Wrong-way / loss-frame contrast** ("back test once… two weeks later bleeding money") makes the correct method feel earned.
5. **Escalating numbers** as proof stacking (300 sub-agents → 80% cheaper → $10M revenue).
6. **Concrete before→after swaps** ("Instead of 'manage the team' it becomes 'reduced customer churn by 18%…'") — the single most memorable retention device; it shows, doesn't tell.
7. **The gate itself** ("Nothing gets passed until everything clears the gate") doubles as a metaphor that keeps the multi-agent logic vivid.

## 7. Proof & Credibility Style

- **Real, checkable numbers over vibes:** install counts (277,000), GitHub stars (96,000), question counts (60), pricing ($99/attempt, $5–7K/project).
- **Borrowed authority via institutions:** Deloitte, Google, Meta, Fortune 500, "the format people who landed roles at… actually use." He never says "trust me"; he says "this is what the winners already do."
- **Third-person receipts, not first-person flex** (this is why he fits Alex): "there's a team running 20 AI agents… to generate their clients 10 million dollars," "Deloitte is paying thousands of employees." The success belongs to a *them*, and you're being shown the machine.
- **Named tools/models as specificity signals:** Opus 4, Sonnet, Kimi K2, MCP servers (GitHub / Postgres / Supabase / Slack), Corey Haines' marketing skills, ATS, Google XYZ formula. Specificity = credibility.
- **The only first person is the closing "I made a guide"** — offering the asset, not narrating a personal journey.

## 8. CTA Formula

**Verbatim skeleton (8 of 8, near-identical):**
> "I [created / built / made / put together] a detailed guide on how to [build this out / the exact prompts]. Comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send it to you. Follow for more advice on making money and running your business with tech and AI."

**Keyword mechanics:** always a **single word that echoes the topic** — the topic IS the keyword:
- V1 → `dev` · V2 → (agency/`start`) · V3 → `hire` · V4 → `skill` · V5 → `test` · V6 → `quant` · V7 → `script` · V8 → `prompt`

The guide/prompts are **always gated behind the comment**; the VO describes what each prompt/agent *does* but never reads the prompt text aloud. The keyword is short, typeable, and topic-obvious so it's frictionless.

## 9. Why It Works (transferable mechanism)

- **Insider-gap hook** ("most people don't realize") sells belonging: cross this line and you're ahead of everyone. Cheapest possible curiosity gap, endlessly reusable.
- **Named steps convert a vague system into a memorable object** you can picture ("The Scanner," "The Surgeon"). Naming = retention + shareability.
- **Every step carries a why** so it teaches instead of listing, which earns the watch-time and the save.
- **Numbers do the persuading** — outcomes are quantified, so the value feels real and de-risked without a single testimonial video.
- **The gate is baked into the value** — he shows you the architecture (which is genuinely useful) and withholds only the copy-paste prompts, so the comment CTA feels like a fair trade, not a bait.
