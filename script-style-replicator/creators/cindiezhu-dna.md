> Source: 8 transcripts, 2,280–292,400 views (median ~2–7K, so 292K/25K/12K are the true outliers), fetched 2026-07-12. Reconciles + upgrades `memory/cindiezhu-style-reference.md` with verbatim transcript evidence. Where they differ, transcripts win (noted inline).

## 0. Voiceprint
A warm, fast "let me put you on" friend who opens every reel with **"Did you know Claude can now ___?"**, kills your objection before you raise it (**"before you spend thousands… try this"**), then walks you through one complete no-code loop in numbered, "okay so"-glued steps where every feature is instantly re-translated into a plain outcome (**"which means…"**), and lands on a one-word comment gate.

## 1. Opener / Hook Formula

### Primary template — the "Did you know … can now" novelty question (5 of 8)
```
Did you know [that] [Claude / you] can now [DO IMPRESSIVE OUTCOME] [with just N tools/skills]?
```
The engine is **novelty + capability + a number**, phrased as a question so it reads as a curiosity gap on the mute-readable title card. The value noun ("creative studio", "resume", "website") always lands by ~word 8–12.

Verbatim (all 5):
- **"Did you know that you can now build an entire creative studio inside of Claude with just seven skills?"** — 7 Claude skills (292K)
- **"Did you know that Claude can now make your resume basically unrejectable?"** — 4 Skills resume (25.6K)
- **"Did you know that Claude can now be a full-time interior designer?"** — Nanobanana house (12.3K)
- **"Did you know that Claude can now design perfect Instagram carousels without you touching any design tool?"** — carousels (7.5K)
- **"Did you know you can now build a $10,000 website with just two tools?"** — $10k website (5.8K)

The word **"now"** is the load-bearing novelty flag — it appears in all 5 primary openers and in variant #8 ("now you can"). It signals "this is newly possible," the core outlier driver.

### Variant openers (the other 3)
- **Rhetorical "Have you ever … wish you could" desire hook:** *"Have you ever landed on a website that you absolutely loved and you just thought, wow, I wish I could make something like this? Well, now you can."* — recreate any site (2.2K). Same job as "Did you know," routed through a shared desire.
- **First-person result cold-open (highest-energy):** *"I just let Claude run overnight and it turned out 50 studio-level ad creatives in the morning."* — overnight assets (5.6K). Leads with the receipt, no question. (This is the one Alex must rewrite — see §10.)
- **Declarative provocation / contrarian slam:** *"Claude is the biggest yes man on the internet. It'll tell you your ideas are amazing, even if it has a fatal flaw."* — sparring partner (2.7K). Names a shared frustration, then fixes it.

### The 3–5 rules that make the hook fire
1. **Novelty first.** The word "now" / "can now" reframes a known tool as newly capable. This is the single most repeated device (6 of 8).
2. **A hard number in the hook.** "seven skills", "four skills", "$10,000", "two tools", "50 assets", "N tools." Never "some" or "a few" in the hook.
3. **Outcome, not mechanism.** The hook promises a *result you want* (unrejectable resume, $10k website, full creative studio), never the method (never "MCP", "subagents", "ATS parser" in line 1).
4. **Value noun by ~word 12, zero jargon** — passes the 12-year-old test every time ("resume", "website", "house", "carousels").
5. **Question or provocation, not a statement** in 6 of 8 — it creates the open loop the body then closes.

### 5 verbatim opener lines (cite video)

2. "Did you know that Claude can now make your resume basically unrejectable?" (25.6K)

## 2. Structural Skeleton (the beat map)

**Two shapes, both ~140–240 words (one 312-word deep-dive).** Weighted by views, the **Listicle** shape dominates the outliers (292K, 25.6K).

### Shape A — LISTICLE ("N skills/personas") — her biggest hits
```
1. HOOK          "Did you know … can now … with just N [skills]?"      (~15 words)
2. OBJECTION     "So before you [waste money/time/effort], try this."   (~15 words)
3. ITEMS ×N      "Number one, [NAME]. So this [does X], which means [OUTCOME]."  (~25 words each)
4. RECAP LOOP    "So that's the whole loop. [verb, verb, verb, verb]."  (optional)
5. CTA           "Comment [KEYWORD] … full setup guide for the N."
```

### Shape B — TUTORIAL / follow-along ("do this first") — the how-to reels
```
1. HOOK          "Did you know Claude can now [be a PERSONA]?"
2. OBJECTION     "So before you [expensive alternative], do this first."
3. SETUP STEPS   "OK so first, [go here], [click this], and then [paste this]."  (glued with "okay so", "and then")
4. THE FUN PART  "OK so this is the fun part. [the payoff action]."     (retention re-hook)
5. PROOF/AWE     "Now Claude will [result]. It's insane."
6. CTA           "Comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send you the full guide."
```

### The section-label grammar
Her list items are named as **persona nouns with a definite article** ("the diagnosis / the recruiter / the rewriter / the hiring manager") OR **plain feature names announced by ordinal** ("Number one, re-motion. / Number two, competitor ads extractor."). The article + noun turns an abstract feature into a character with a job — the #1 steal from the old ref, confirmed by transcript.

### Ordered beats, each FUNCTION + verbatim example
- **HOOK** — open the novelty loop. *"Did you know that Claude can now make your resume basically unrejectable?"*
- **OBJECTION-PREEMPT (the "before you") — 6 of 8.** Frame the costly wrong way, then offer the shortcut. *"So before you send another resume into the void and wonder why no one's calling you back… try this."* / *"So before you repaint, rearrange, or drop $3,000 on new furniture, do this first."*
- **ITEM / STEP with instant outcome-translation.** *"Number two, competitor ads extractor. So this skill pulls live competitor ads, which means all their messaging, hooks, and CTAs that are working right now."*
- **FRICTION STRIP (spoken aloud).** *"No editing needed."* / *"you don't have to be a developer."* / *"and that's literally it."*
- **RECAP LOOP.** *"So that's the whole loop. Diagnose, score, rewrite, and prep."* / *"So overall, five of these are completely free."*
- **CTA.** *"Comment skills if you want the full install guide for all five."*

### Target length / word budget
- Listicle: **200–240 words**, N=4–7 items, ~25 words/item.
- Tutorial: **140–200 words** (deep-dive up to 312).
- Openers: **~15 words** (range 9–24).

## 4. Sentence Rhythm & Mechanics (measured from the 8 transcripts, 1,686 words total)

- **Avg sentence length:** ~13–16 words. Short, breath-sized, declarative. Longest sentences are the outcome-translator run-ons chained with "so" and "which means."
- **Opener length:** avg **~15 words** (measured: 18/12/12/16/14/17/9/24).
- **Imperative density: very high in the body.** The step/CTA sections are dense with commands: "try this," "do this first," "go to Claude," "click projects," "paste this prompt," "type in your topic," "comment banana." Roughly every step sentence is an imperative. Hooks and outcome-translations are declarative; steps are imperative — a clean declarative-hook → imperative-body split.
- **Number / $ density: high.** In ~1,686 words: "seven skills," "four skills," "$10,000," "$3,000," "two tools," "50 assets/ad creatives," "1,000 similar job descriptions," "1,000 hrs"(ref), "/10," "5 inspo photos," "12 months," "three biggest cracks," "Google XYZ (X/Y/Z)," "month worth," "in one minute," "in a few minutes." Roughly one hard number every 25–35 words. Every hook carries at least one.
- **Causal connectors: "so" is the dominant glue** — it opens the objection beat, most list items, and the recap ("So before you…", "So this skill…", "So overall…", "So that's the whole loop"). **"and then"** is the sequencing glue in tutorials. **"which means" / "so that" / "so"** are the outcome-translators. **"but before you"** flips objection to solution (video 7).
- **Signature punctuation / cadence:** conversational fillers as beat-markers — **"Okay,"/"OK so"** to start a new step, **"literally"** as her top intensifier (7+ uses: "just seven skills," "literally generating," "that's literally it," "literally takes a few minutes," "It's literally two tools"), **"honestly"** and **"basically"/"in summary"** to preface the recap. Frequent **"and that's all" / "and that's literally it"** to signal a step is trivially done.

## 6. Retention / Escalation Devices

- **"OK so this is the fun part."** (videos 3 + 8) — an explicit re-hook right before the payoff step, resetting attention at the midpoint.
- **The running number.** Each list item is a fresh open loop ("Number one… Number two…"), so the count itself pulls viewers to the end. The hook's "N skills" is a promise the body pays off item by item.
- **Outcome-translation as a mini-reward.** Every feature is immediately cashed out into a benefit ("which means…"), so no beat feels like dry spec — each one delivers a hit.
- **Escalating awe verbs.** "It's insane," "It's pretty insane," "Claude does everything," "honestly the best there is" — punctuation of amazement that keeps energy climbing.
- **Friction-strip relief beats.** "No editing needed," "you don't have to be a developer" — each removes a reason to bounce.
- **Bonus / aside beats.** "And by the way, Claude can actually generate your brand guidelines too" — an unpromised extra that rewards staying.
- **The recap loop.** "So that's the whole loop: diagnose, score, rewrite, prep" — compresses the whole video into one memorable rhythm right before the CTA (aids saves/rewatches).

## 7. Proof & Credibility Style

- **Third-person social receipts (this is her credibility spine, and it's Alex-safe):**
  - "People are literally generating a month worth of carousels in just a few minutes."
  - "So many agencies, content creators and marketers are generating hundreds of UGC ads while they sleep."
  - "Most people are just pasting their resume into ChatGPT and saying, make this better. That's why it's not working."
- **Authority name-drops kept true and general:** "Meta, Google, and Fortune 500 roles," "the Google XYZ formula (Accomplished X as measured by Y by doing Z)," "a real applicant tracking system." Named systems/companies as the standard, never a fabricated quote.
- **Concrete numbers as proof of scale:** "analyzes your resume against 1,000 similar job descriptions," "50 studio-level ad creatives," "rates your answers out of 10."
- **Show-the-real-thing (from the old ref, still true):** she cuts to the actual artifact — real resume with highlighted keywords, live app UI, the finished website preview. Claims are always backed by the on-screen thing.
- **Cost anchors as proof of value:** "$10,000 website," "$3,000 on new furniture," "pay an agency thousands" — frames the free/cheap method against an expensive baseline.

## 8. CTA Formula

**Verbatim template:**
```
Comment [ONE-WORD KEYWORD] [if you want] and I'll send you the full [setup / install] guide [for the N thing].
```

**Keyword mechanics:**
- **7 of 8 videos** end on a one-word comment gate. The word is always the topic's memorable noun: **skills · resume · banana · build · MCP**. (The 8th, carousels 7.5K, has no gate — it ends on a benefit line "You don't have to use a design tool ever again." Note: this was her lowest-CTA reel; the gate is the norm.)
- Keyword is short, on-theme, easy to type — maximizes comment volume (the old ref logged 20–34K comments).
- The gate promises the **gated deliverable**: "the full PDF guide," "the prompts that I use," "the full install guide," "the full setup guide." The reel shows *enough to believe it works*; the complete word-for-word system is DM-gated.
- Her CTA has a **tail after the keyword** ("…and I'll send you the full setup guide") — Alex trims this (see §10).

## 9. Why It Works (transferable mechanism)

1. **Novelty question + a number** opens a curiosity loop in the first breath and reads on mute. "Can now" makes a familiar tool feel newly powerful.
2. **Objection-preempt ("before you waste $X, try this")** disarms skepticism and anchors the free method against an expensive baseline — the viewer feels they're being saved money/time.
3. **Persona-naming + instant outcome-translation** ("the recruiter… which means…") converts every technical feature into a character with a job and a felt benefit — zero jargon, maximum "I need this."
4. **One complete, doable loop** with friction stripped out loud ("no code," "a few minutes") makes the outcome feel achievable *right now*, driving saves.
5. **One-word comment gate** converts curiosity into the algorithm's favorite signal (comments) while gating the real work behind a DM.

## 10. Adapting to Alex (the fusion layer)

### What transfers cleanly to faceless mascot + Alex's rules
- **The "Did you know Claude can now ___ with just N ___?" hook** — perfect for a title card + clay-mascot reveal, mute-readable, number-forward. Adopt wholesale.
- **The objection-preempt "before you [expensive way], try this"** — pure value-framing, no first person needed. Adopt wholesale.
- **Persona-naming + "which means" outcome-translation** — this IS the no-jargon engine Alex wants ("turn Claude into a team of 4 specialists" not "subagent orchestration"). Adopt wholesale.
- **Third-person social receipts** ("agencies are generating hundreds of UGC ads while they sleep") — already anecdote-free, already Alex-legal. Adopt wholesale.
- **Friction-strip beats + recap loop + one-word gate.** Adopt.

### Conflicts with Alex's HARD RULES → exact resolution
| Cindy habit | Alex HARD RULE | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| First-person result cold-open: *"I just let Claude run overnight and woke up to 50 assets."* | **No first-person anecdote** | Convert to third-person receipt / novelty question: *"Did you know Claude can now run overnight and hand you 50 studio-level ads by morning?"* or *"People are letting Claude run overnight and waking up to 50 finished ads."* |
| Asides like *"the prompts that I use," "how I set this up," "It's pretty insane what I can build."* | **No first-person anecdote** | Drop the "I." Keep the awe as a general beat: *"the exact prompts that make this work," "It's insane what this builds."* |
| Full step-by-step install shown on screen (video 3 shows the whole setup) | **Gate the how** | Allowed as a *rough follow-along* — show partial/spoken steps as the value, but the complete word-for-word prompt set + PDF stays behind the keyword. Never narrate the full copy-paste system end to end; name the artifact and sell the result. |
| CTA tail: *"…and I'll send you the full setup guide."* | **Cut on the keyword** | End hard on the keyword. *"Comment AGENTS."* Not *"Comment AGENTS and I'll send it over."* |
| Occasional em-dash cadence in ASR | **No em-dashes** | Use commas, periods, colons only. |
| Buzzword risk if she names a raw tool | **Zero jargon, 12-yr-old test** | Always route through persona + outcome. "MCP" becomes "one connection you set up once, then Claude films forever." |

### Gate-the-how mapping (this creator's format)
- **SHOWN (sold):** the outcome, the persona lineup, the plain-English "which means" benefits, the friction strips, one rough spoken step or two, the on-screen artifact/receipt.
- **GATED (keyword):** the full prompt text, the complete install/setup sequence, the PDF/system, the exact copy-paste blocks.
